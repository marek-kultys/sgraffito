The word “sgraffito” comes from the Italian “graffiare” which means “to scratch”.

The technique of sgraffito

Sgraffito mural is made by applying several layers of tinted plaster one on top of another, onto a wettened masonry or concrete wall. Then selected parts of front layers are removed by scratching to reveal coloured layers underneath into a designed image. Because a sgraffito mural is made of plaster, it provides both protection for the wall and unique decoration for the space.

Sgraffito murals are known for their durability and versatility. Depending on the context, space, environment, and your own preferences sgraffito murals can be adapted for virtually any situation.

  • Most masonry & concrete walls

Well Known Sgraffito Murals by Other Artists

Sgraffito is known to have been used since the classical antiquity. It was revived and popularised during the Renaissance age. Today few artists master the technique, so every new sgraffito mural is not only unique for its design, but also rare for its technique. Here are some well known examples of sgraffito art by acclaimed mural painters from around the world that I admire.

Sgraffito mural in St. Benet’s Chaplaincy in London by Adam Kossowski (1964).
Sgraffito on a coffee house facade in Warsaw Old Town by Jan Zamoyski (1953).
Art Nouveau sgraffito on the Cauchie house in Brussels by Paul Cauchie (1905).
Medieval sgraffito decoration on houses in the village of Pyrgi, Greece.
Sgraffito mural on the Koch Seminar Building in Aspen by Herbert Beyer (1953).
Sgraffito decoration on the Jagiellonian University students’ house in Kraków.
Sgraffito decoration on The House at the Minute facade in Prague (17th Century).
Sgraffito facade in Warsaw Old Town (1953).
Sgraffito on a transformer station in Gransee, Germany, by Eberhard Werner (1956).
